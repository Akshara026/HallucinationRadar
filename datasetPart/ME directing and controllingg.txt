3.1 Relation between motivation and needs
Unsatisfied Need: Motivation begins when an individual feels a need that is not fulfilled (such as need for money, recognition, or security).

Tension: The unsatisfied need creates tension or discomfort within the individual.

Drives: This tension leads to drives, which are inner forces that push the person to act in order to reduce the tension.

Behaviour: Drives result in goal-directed behaviour. The individual takes action to satisfy the need (for example, working harder to earn rewards).

Performance: The behaviour leads to a certain level of performance, depending on the effort and ability of the individual.

Reward: Performance is followed by rewards, which may be monetary (salary, bonus) or non-monetary (praise, promotion).

Need Satisfaction or Dissatisfaction: If the reward meets the need, the need is satisfied. If not, the individual feels dissatisfied.

Reduction or Increase in Tension: If the need is satisfied, tension is reduced. If the need remains unsatisfied, tension increases, and the cycle of motivation starts again.

3.2 Abraham Maslowâ€™s Hierarchy of Needs â€“ Explanation

Abraham Maslow proposed that human behaviour is motivated by needs, and these needs are arranged in a hierarchy based on their importance. According to him, people try to satisfy lower-level needs first, and once these are reasonably satisfied, they move towards higher-level needs.

Step 1: Physiological Needs

These are the basic survival needs of human life.
They arise from the physiology of the body.

Examples:

Food ,Water, Air, Shelter, Sleep

 These needs are the most urgent.
 If these needs are not satisfied, a person cannot think of anything else.

Example:
A hungry person will focus only on getting food and will not think about social status or self-development.

Step 2: Safety / Security Needs

Once physiological needs are satisfied, people seek security and protection.

Examples: Job security, Stable income, Protection from physical harm, Safe working conditions

 These needs create a feeling of freedom from fear and threat.
 Both economic security and physical safety are included.

Step 3: Social Needs

After safety needs are fulfilled, people desire social relationships.

Examples: Love and affection, Friendship, Belongingness, Acceptance by others
Humans are social beings and want to associate with others.
Lack of social acceptance can lead to loneliness and dissatisfaction.

Step 4: Esteem Needs

These needs relate to self-respect and recognition from others.

Examples: Respect, Status, Prestige, Recognition

Feeling of dominance or achievement

 These needs are also called egoistic needs.
 Fulfilment of esteem needs increases confidence and self-worth.

Step 5: Self-Actualization (Self-Fulfillment) Needs

This is the highest level of Maslowâ€™s hierarchy.

These needs relate to realising oneâ€™s full potential and becoming what one is capable of becoming.

Examples:

A musician creating music, A poet writing poetry, A person pursuing creativity, growth, and personal excellence

 It is about personal growth and fulfillment, not material rewards.
 Very few people reach this level completely.

3.2.1 Maslowâ€™s Core Assumptions

Human needs are arranged in a hierarchy of importance.
A satisfied need no longer motivates behaviour.
Once a lower-level need is satisfied, the next higher need becomes dominant.

Example:
A starving person will not be interested in yoga, meditation, social approval, or prestige. Only after hunger is satisfied will higher needs become important.

Limitations of Maslowâ€™s Theory
1. Needs do not always follow a strict hierarchy

People may seek higher-level needs even when lower-level needs are not fully satisfied.

Example:
An artist may pursue creativity despite financial insecurity.

2. Needs vary across countries and cultures
The priority of needs differs from one society to another.

Example:
Workers in Spain and Belgium may value esteem needs more than security needs.

3. Human behaviour is motivated by multiple needs
People may be motivated by several needs at the same time, not just one.
 This contradicts Maslowâ€™s assumption that needs are satisfied one at a time.

3.4 Theory X and Theory Y
  According to Douglas McGregor managers hold two sets of 
assumptions about their subordinates. Mangers assume that their 
subordinates belong to either of two categories called category X 
and category Y

McGregor proposed that theory X and theory Y are 
important tools in understanding human behaviour 
at work and designing work environment. Neither 
the two sets of assumptions are applicable in all 
situations. But theory X is more applicable to 
unskilled and uneducated lower level employees 
who may be working for satisfaction of lower level 
needs.Theory Y is applicable to educated, skilled 
and professional employees who are capable of

3.4.1Theory X Assumptions

Theory X is based on a traditional and pessimistic view of employees. According to this theory, managers believe that employees naturally dislike work and will try to avoid it whenever possible. Because of this attitude, employees need to be closely supervised, directed, and controlled. Managers using Theory X assume that workers must be threatened with punishment or strict discipline to ensure that work is completed.

Further, Theory X assumes that employees avoid responsibility, prefer to be directed, and do not want accountability. Such employees are believed to lack ambition and mainly seek job security above everything else. As a result, management relies heavily on authority, control, and coercion to achieve organisational goals.

3.4.2 Theory Y Assumptions

Theory Y presents a modern and optimistic view of employees. It assumes that employees enjoy work, just as they enjoy play or rest, when the working conditions are favourable. According to this theory, people are capable of self-direction and self-control if they are committed to organisational objectives.

Theory Y also assumes that employees accept and even seek responsibility, and they are capable of handling responsibilities at all levels of the organisation. It believes that creativity and problem-solving abilities are widely distributed among employees, including those at lower levels. Therefore, employees can participate in decision-making and contribute innovative ideas.

Managers following Theory Y focus on motivation, trust, participation, and empowerment rather than strict control.

6.1 Purpose of Control

Control is an important managerial function that helps the organization achieve its goals efficiently and effectively. The main purposes of control are explained below:

1. Adapt to Environmental Changes

The business environment is dynamic and continuously changing due to factors such as technology, competition, government policies, and customer preferences. Control helps the organization identify these changes early and take corrective actions to adapt accordingly.

2. Limit the Accumulation of Errors

Control helps in detecting mistakes at an early stage. By identifying deviations from standards in time, management can take corrective actions and prevent small errors from becoming major problems.

3. Cope with Organizational Complexity

As organizations grow in size and operations become more complex, it becomes difficult for managers to monitor all activities directly. Control systems help management coordinate various departments and activities, ensuring smooth functioning of the organization.

4. Minimize Costs

Through effective control, wastage of resources can be reduced. It ensures optimum utilization of resources, avoids unnecessary expenses, and helps in cost reduction, thereby improving profitability.

6.4.Types of Control

Managers can exercise control at three different stages of an activity: before, during, and after the activity is completed. Accordingly, there are three types of control:

1. Feedforward Control (Control Before the Activity)

Feedforward control is exercised before the actual activity begins. Its main purpose is to anticipate problems and prevent them from occurring.

It focuses on inputs such as raw materials, manpower, and resources.

It ensures that the right inputs are available in the right quantity and quality.

Example:
Checking the quality of raw materials before production begins.

2. Concurrent Control (Control During the Activity)

Concurrent control is exercised while the activity is in progress. It helps in correcting problems as they occur.

It focuses on processes.

It ensures that activities are performed according to plan in real time.

Example:
A supervisor monitoring workers on the production line to ensure proper work methods are followed.

3. Feedback Control (Control After the Activity)

Feedback control is exercised after the activity has been completed. It helps in correcting problems after they occur and improving future performance.

It focuses on output.

6.5. Comparison of Past-Oriented and Future-Oriented Controls

Control systems can be classified based on the time at which corrective action is taken. Accordingly, they are divided into past-oriented controls and future-oriented controls.

1. Past-Oriented Control (Feedback Control)

Past-oriented control focuses on results that have already been achieved. It is exercised after the activity is completed.

It is based on outputs and performance reports.

Information about actual performance is collected and compared with standards.

Corrective actions are taken after deviations occur.

It helps management learn from past mistakes and improve future planning.

Example:
Reviewing sales performance at the end of the month and taking corrective steps for the next month.

2. Future-Oriented Control (Feedforward Control)

Future-oriented control focuses on preventing problems before they occur. It is exercised before the activity begins.

It concentrates on inputs and processes.

It anticipates potential problems and takes preventive actions.

It ensures that deviations do not occur in the first place.

Example:
Selecting qualified employees and ensuring proper training before work begins.

Past-oriented control uses feedback from outputs to improve future actions.

Future-oriented control uses feedforward information from inputs and processes to prevent errors.
